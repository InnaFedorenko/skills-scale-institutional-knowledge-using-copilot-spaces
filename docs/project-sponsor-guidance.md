# Project Sponsor Guidance

This document provides concise guidance for **Project Sponsors** on their role, responsibilities, and recommended practices within OctoAcme project delivery.

> **Audience:** Project Sponsors, Project Managers  
> **Related roles:** [octoacme-roles-and-personas.md](octoacme-roles-and-personas.md)  
> **Related documents:** [change-control-process.md](change-control-process.md)

---

## What Is a Project Sponsor?

A Project Sponsor is an executive or senior leader who:

- **Champions** the project within the organisation, ensuring it receives priority and resources
- **Empowers** the Project Manager to make day-to-day decisions
- **Intervenes** on strategic or organisational issues that the Project Manager cannot resolve alone
- **Approves** major decisions on scope, budget, and resources

The Sponsor does not manage the project day-to-day—that is the Project Manager's job. The Sponsor's value is in removing high-level blockers, making go/no-go decisions, and keeping the project aligned with organisational goals.

---

## Key Responsibilities

- Approve the project charter and any significant changes to it
- Allocate or unlock budget and resources when requested by the Project Manager
- Resolve cross-organisational dependencies or blockers
- Provide strategic guidance when the project scope or direction needs adjustment
- Attend scheduled milestone reviews and sign off on major deliverables
- Act as the final escalation point for unresolved issues

---

## Supporting Escalation

When the Project Manager escalates an issue to the Sponsor, the Sponsor should:

1. **Acknowledge** the escalation within 1 business day
2. **Understand** the issue: review the Project Manager's summary and ask clarifying questions if needed
3. **Decide or delegate**: make the required decision, or identify and engage the right person to resolve it
4. **Communicate** the outcome back to the Project Manager with a clear decision and any conditions
5. **Follow up** to confirm the blocker was resolved at the next check-in

### Escalation Triggers (when to expect an escalation)

| Situation | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Resource conflict | Another team is pulling a key contributor away from the project |
| Budget overrun risk | Forecast exceeds approved budget by more than 10% |
| Strategic scope change | A stakeholder requests a significant change to project objectives |
| Blocked dependency | A third-party or partner team is unresponsive and blocking progress |
| Go/no-go decision | A major release requires Sponsor sign-off |

---

## Approving Major Scope and Resource Decisions

Major decisions require explicit Sponsor approval before the Project Manager can proceed. The Project Manager will prepare a brief decision memo that includes:

- **Context**: what the decision is and why it is needed
- **Options**: at least two options with pros/cons and a recommendation
- **Impact**: schedule, budget, and risk implications of each option
- **Request**: the specific action required from the Sponsor (approve, reject, or defer)

The Sponsor should aim to respond within **2 business days** for standard decisions and **same day** for critical or time-sensitive decisions.

Decisions involving significant scope or budget changes should be logged in the [change-control-process.md](change-control-process.md) change log.

---

## Recommended Cadence of Sponsor Check-Ins

| Cadence | Forum | Purpose |
|---------|-------|---------|
| **Monthly** | 30-minute 1:1 with Project Manager | Review status, risks, and upcoming decisions |
| **At each milestone** | Milestone review meeting | Sign off on deliverables and confirm next-phase go/no-go |
| **As needed** | Ad-hoc call or async decision memo | Respond to escalations or urgent decisions |
| **At project close** | Retrospective or close-out meeting | Review outcomes against charter goals; capture lessons learned |

### Tips for Effective Sponsor Engagement

- **Keep it brief**: the Project Manager should bring focused summaries, not lengthy reports
- **Be decisive**: timely decisions prevent downstream delays for the team
- **Maintain visibility**: review the weekly status update email/dashboard before each monthly check-in
- **Be an advocate**: actively promote the project's value to peers and leadership to help maintain organisational support

## Decision Authority Matrix

| Decision Type | Project Manager | Project Sponsor |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Day-to-day task prioritisation | ✅ Decides | ℹ️ Informed |
| Minor scope adjustments (no baseline impact) | ✅ Decides | ℹ️ Informed |
| Standard change requests (minor baseline impact) | ✅ Approves | ℹ️ Informed |
| Major scope or schedule changes | 📋 Recommends | ✅ Approves |
| Budget increases | 📋 Recommends | ✅ Approves |
| Strategic direction changes | 📋 Recommends | ✅ Approves |
| Go/no-go for major releases | 📋 Recommends | ✅ Approves |
| Project cancellation | 📋 Recommends | ✅ Decides |

## Example Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Budget Escalation

The Project Manager identifies that the project is forecast to exceed its approved budget by 15% due to an unplanned dependency on a third-party service. The PM prepares a two-option decision memo (absorb from contingency vs. reduce scope) and sends it to the Sponsor. The Sponsor reviews within one business day, selects the scope-reduction option, and communicates this to the PM, who updates the project plan and notifies stakeholders.

### Scenario 2: Go/No-Go for a Major Release

Two weeks before a major release, the Project Manager requests Sponsor sign-off. The PM shares a release readiness report covering test results, open risks, and stakeholder approvals. The Sponsor reviews the report, asks one clarifying question about a high-severity risk, receives a satisfactory answer, and provides written go-ahead via email. The Release Coordinator then proceeds with the release schedule.
